The room is white. The room has always been white. The whiteness is not the cold whiteness of a hospital but the soft cream of an old book, which is a thing the patient remembers without remembering that she remembers it.

In the corner of the room there is a chair. The chair is the same color as the room. There is a person in the chair sometimes. The person is not always the same person but the person is often the same person. The person has hands.

The hands do things. The hands change the sheet. The hands hold a cup of water to the patient's mouth and the patient drinks and the water tastes like water. The hands turn the patient gently every two hours and the patient feels the turn but does not feel resistance to it. The hands write things in a book the patient cannot see. The hands wear a ring that is silver and that the patient has decided is important, although the patient does not know why.

The patient does not have a name. She had a name once. She knows she had a name because the absence of it is shaped like something. The shape is *Anna*. She does not know that the shape is *Anna*. She knows only that there is a shape.

She is humming.

She has been humming for seven years.

---

The hands belong to a woman. The woman is older than the patient — the patient knows this without checking, the way one knows the weight of a coin in one's pocket — but the woman is not old. The woman has gray at the temples. The woman has the kind of face the patient might have liked to talk to, in the time before.

The woman comes in the morning and the evening. The woman comes at other times too but the patient is mostly asleep then. The woman opens the curtains in the morning and closes them in the evening. The morning light is the same as the evening light because the windows face an inner courtyard. The patient has not seen the sun in some long time.

The woman says, sometimes, *good morning,* and the patient says, sometimes, *good morning,* though the patient is not certain what time it is and the woman does not correct her.

The woman says, sometimes, *can you sing it for me*, and the patient hums, and the woman closes her eyes for the length of the hum and then opens them and writes something in the book.

The patient does not know what she is humming. The patient is humming what she has always been humming. There is only one song. There has only ever been one song.

---

The woman is kind to the patient.

The patient knows this in the way she knows the weight of the coin. The kindness is structural. The kindness is the room being warm. The kindness is the water being at the right temperature. The kindness is the way the woman lifts the patient under her arms, not by her wrists, when she helps her sit up. The kindness is the way the woman never asks the patient who she is or where she comes from, the way the other ones used to.

The kindness is the way the woman does not flinch when the patient hums.

---

Sometimes the patient remembers a boy.

The boy is small. The boy has hair the color of weak tea. The boy is eating something — a piece of bread, a piece of fruit, the patient cannot quite see — at a table the patient is also sitting at. The patient is holding a cup. The patient is laughing. The boy is laughing. There is light from a window the patient cannot see.

The memory is bright. The memory is unrolled. The memory is sharper than anything else the patient has, sharper than the room itself, sharper than the woman's face, sharper than the humming. The memory has the quality of a photograph the patient has stared at for many years.

The patient does not trust the memory. The memory is too clean.

This is what the binding has done, although the patient does not know that, and could not put it into the words even if she did: the binding has made certain memories into the kind of thing one cannot doubt. They are not real. They are not unreal. They are completed. They are the memories the binding completed, and they have the texture of completed memories, which is the texture of something that no longer admits revision.

The boy at the table is one of those memories. The patient knows this in the way she knows everything now, which is incompletely.

She does not know whether the boy is hers.

She thinks he is.

---

The woman comes in the afternoon today. The afternoon is unusual. The woman usually does not come in the afternoon. The woman is carrying something.

"I brought you flowers," the woman says.

The patient looks at the flowers. They are small and white. The patient does not know what they are. The woman puts them in a glass on the table beside the bed.

"It's your birthday," the woman says.

The patient hums.

The woman sits down in the chair. She looks at the flowers. She looks at the patient. She looks at the flowers again.

"I'm not supposed to tell you that it's your birthday," the woman says. "It's against protocol. But I thought you should know."

The patient stops humming.

The patient looks at the woman.

For a moment, for the length of a held breath — the case file will not record this moment because there is no biometric instrument that records the gap between humming — the patient sees the woman clearly. The woman has a son. The son is grown now. The son has hair the color of weak tea and is twenty-two years old and is, this afternoon, in another city, processing freedom-of-information requests for the Office of Memetic Containment, which is the job he has had for three months. The woman knows this. The patient does not know this.

The patient sees the woman seeing her.

And the woman, for the length of that breath, sees the patient seeing her — and considers, briefly, the way one considers a step toward the edge of a cliff one will not take, telling the patient who she is and where her son went and what name the patient used to answer to.

The woman does not say any of these things.

The patient resumes humming.

The woman writes nothing in the book.

She sits in the chair for forty more minutes, longer than she should, and then she stands and tucks the blanket more carefully than she needs to around the patient's shoulders and turns off the light and goes home.

The patient is asleep within four minutes. She dreams of a boy at a table. She dreams the dream completely, as she has dreamed it almost every night for fourteen years. The boy laughs. The patient laughs. There is bread on the table. There is fruit. There is light from a window.

In the morning the woman comes back. She opens the curtains. The flowers are still in the glass.

"Good morning," the woman says.

"Good morning," the patient says.

The patient hums.

The woman writes in the book.

The day proceeds.
