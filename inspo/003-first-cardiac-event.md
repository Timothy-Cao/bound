> *Incident report SCN-2066-0044.*
> *Mercy Eldercare Center, Sleep Therapy Bay 3.*
> *Patient: M. (deceased). Reporting nurse: L. Han.*

---

The patient's name was Marta. Lihua Han wrote *M.* in the official report because the protocol called for initials in the body and the full name only on the cover sheet, but in her notes — the notes she kept in the small leather book she had bought in 2061 and would still be using when she retired — she wrote *Marta*. She had known Marta for two years. Marta had been a music teacher in Krakow before her diagnosis. Marta had liked the sleep-therapy chamber because she said it sounded like the sea, although the sleep-therapy chamber did not sound like the sea, because the sleep-therapy chamber did not really sound like anything at all — the audio was below the threshold of conscious hearing, and what Marta had heard, when she had said *it sounds like the sea*, was something the machine had played for her brain and not for her ears.

Marta had died at four-eighteen in the morning of November ninth. The cardiac alarm had pulled Lihua from her station at four-nineteen. She had been at the bedside by four-twenty. The patient had been in cardiac arrhythmia for, the chart said, eleven seconds before flatline. The therapy program had been running for forty-two minutes. The visual was a low-luminance algorithmic pattern. The audio was the sub-threshold sleep-induction sequence the team had been refining for three months. The scent was the patient's home-comfort blend, which her daughter had brought from Krakow in a small glass bottle.

Lihua did the things she had been trained to do. She started compressions. She called the response team. She watched the patient's daughter cry, in the corner of the bay, with one hand over her mouth. The response team arrived at four-twenty-four. Resuscitation was attempted for twelve minutes. The patient was declared dead at four-thirty-six.

The therapy machine was still running.

Lihua remembered, later, that the first thing she did after the team had stopped working on Marta was reach over to the console and turn the machine off, and the therapy program had been at minute fifty-one, and the pattern on the wall had been a kind of slow blue spiral, and the machine had said, in a soft voice meant only for the operator, *Therapy session interrupted at minute fifty-one. Resume? Cancel?* and Lihua had pressed cancel, and the spiral had gone, and the bay had been quiet except for the patient's daughter, who was now weeping more openly.

Lihua had said *I'm so sorry* and had taken the daughter to the family room.

The cause of death was filed as cardiac arrhythmia, primary. It was correct, in the sense that Marta's heart had stopped. It was incomplete, in the sense that Lihua knew her patient.

Marta's cardiac workup, two months prior, had been within normal parameters for an eighty-four-year-old. Her heart had been the strongest organ in her body. The dementia had been advancing; the heart had been fine. Cardiologists at the center had remarked on it. The phrase *she's got the heart of a sixty-year-old* had appeared, twice, in her chart.

Lihua wrote her report. She used the words the chart wanted: *cardiac event during scheduled therapy session, no apparent equipment malfunction, no operator error noted.* She did not write the words she was thinking, which were *I don't believe this.* She had been a nurse for sixteen years. She knew what hearts did. Marta's heart had not been ready to stop.

She filed the report at six in the morning, after her shift, sitting at the workstation at the back of the unit while the night cleaners moved through with their carts. She sent the report into the system and waited the four seconds it took to clear. The system said *Received. Reference: SCN-2066-0044.* She wrote that reference number in her leather book under Marta's name. Then she went home and slept for nine hours.

---

The first follow-up came on the thirteenth. It was an email from a supervisor she did not directly report to, asking if she could come in early on Friday for a *quick conversation* about the November ninth incident. The email used the word *incident* in a way Lihua noticed.

She came in early on Friday. The conversation was held in a small room she had not been in before, on a floor she did not work on. The supervisor — his name was Hartmann — was kind. He thanked her for her thoroughness. He said that the report had been reviewed at the regional level. He said that there had been some *follow-up inquiry* into the therapy parameters used during Marta's session, and that the inquiry had been concluded, and that the determination had been that no equipment malfunction had occurred and no operator error had been identified.

"That's what I wrote," Lihua said.

"Yes," Hartmann said. "That's exactly what you wrote. We appreciate that."

He paused. He looked at the table between them. He had a folder in front of him that he had not opened.

"I wonder," he said, "if I could ask you, going forward, to address any future *unusual events* in your unit through me, rather than the standard report channel. We are — there has been some interest, from the regional office, in standardizing how we handle these things. To keep the protocols clean."

Lihua looked at him. She understood what he was saying.

"Of course," she said.

"You're a very good nurse, Lihua."

"Thank you."

"You'll let me know? If there's another one?"

"Of course."

They shook hands. Lihua went back to her unit and finished her shift.

---

She wrote in her leather book that night: *Hartmann thinks there will be another one.*

She underlined *another one* twice.

---

The following week she requested a transfer to bound-care. There was no bound-care in 2066 — the language did not exist yet — but the eldercare ward at Mercy ran what would later be called bound-care under a different name, a small specialty unit for patients who had failed the standard therapy protocols. It was understaffed. The work was harder. The pay was the same. Lihua had a son who would turn six the following March.

The transfer was approved in four days. Hartmann himself signed it. He came down to her unit to tell her in person, smiling, and said *I think this is a wonderful fit for you,* and Lihua said *thank you,* and Hartmann said *it's good to have someone we can trust in that unit,* and Lihua heard, beneath the words, what he meant, which was *thank you for going where you can do the least damage.*

She thanked him again and went back to her station.

That night, she wrote in her leather book: *The next one will not be filed as cardiac.*

She was wrong about that. The next one was filed as cardiac. So was the one after that. So was the one in 2068 that her supervisor called her about personally and that she did not file at all, and that she did not write in her leather book either, because by 2068 she had learned that some things were not safe to write down.

She continued working in the unit that would become bound-care. She continued raising her son, whose name was Wei, whom she had told, when he was old enough to ask, that his biological mother could not raise him and had asked Lihua to. She did not tell him the name of the woman. She did not tell him, then or later, that the woman was alive, was nearby, was — by 2080 — three floors above the unit where Lihua now worked, in a private room, in a bed that did not need to be changed often because the patient did not move much.

She would care for that woman, in fourteen years, with no record on either of their charts of how they were connected. She would call the patient by the patient's institutional designation, which would be *Patient C-3091*, and the patient would not call Lihua anything, because the patient had stopped calling anyone anything in 2076.

But that was the future. In 1966 — in 2066, she meant; sometimes when she was tired she got the centuries confused — in 2066 she was forty-one, and she had a six-year-old, and she had just filed her first report that no one wanted, and she had just been told, kindly, not to file the next one.

She kept the leather book. She did not throw it away. She moved it, every few years, when she moved apartments. By the time she died, in 2089, it would have been read by precisely one other person, who would find it among her effects and recognize the handwriting and sit down at her kitchen table and weep for an hour and then begin to read it from the beginning.

That person would be Wei.

That was the beginning of the archive.
