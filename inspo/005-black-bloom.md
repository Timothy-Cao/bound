> *Case file 2069-WMK-08-7724.*
> *Subject: unidentified female, est. 32 years.*
> *Exposure: Westmarket Central transit hub, August 4.*
> *Deceased: August 18, 2069.*

---

She is reading the news on her phone at Westmarket Central, platform six, eight-fourteen on a Tuesday morning, and she does not see the image.

She does not see it because she is not looking at the platform screens. She is looking at her phone, which is in safe-mode by default, which means the lead news photo of the morning has loaded as a one-sentence text description: *Sunrise over the Adriatic, Trieste, captured by a freelance photographer.* She does not need to see the photo to know it is beautiful. She believes it is beautiful. She scrolls.

The platform screens are showing what she will later not remember they were showing. A travel advertisement. An advisory about a maintenance closure on the northbound. A municipal art installation that the city has been promoting for six weeks, called *Bloom*, which depicts, on the long horizontal screens above the platform, a slow algorithmic generation of floral patterns that resolve and dissolve in the manner of a stereogram, except that they do not resolve into anything in particular. It is meant to be soothing. The artist's name is on a small placard at the end of the platform, which she has never looked at and will never look at.

The eight-fourteen train arrives. She gets on.

The image is in her field of vision for, the case file will later determine, between eleven and fourteen seconds, depending on her angle of approach. She does not look directly at it. She does not need to. The encoding does not require direct attention. It requires only that the visual field include the pattern long enough for the cortex to register it, which takes about three seconds, and the rest is consolidation.

She rides the train. She gets off at Sankt-Anne, three stops north. She walks to work. She has a normal day. She has a sandwich for lunch. She comes home. She sleeps eight hours.

She is now Marked.

She does not know this. She will not know this.

---

The image was placed on the platform screens at five-fifty-two on the morning of August 4 by a person whose identity will never be established. The placement was achieved through an exploit of the municipal art-installation content management system, a system that was certified safe by a contractor that no longer exists, audited by a body that has since been dissolved, and reviewed for hazardous content by a censor AI that had been trained on a Restricted Stimulus Registry that did not yet contain the relevant pattern, because the pattern was new.

The image displayed for sixty-one minutes before a maintenance technician, responding to an unrelated complaint, noticed that the *Bloom* installation was running a frame she did not recognize and shut down the screens. By the time the screens went dark, an estimated [██████] people had passed through platform six. Most of them, like the woman, were on their phones in safe-mode and never looked up.

This is the only reason civilization survives the next six months.

If the screens had displayed at eye-level, in an enclosed space, with no phones to distract, the casualty count of what will be called Black Bloom would have been not fourteen thousand but two hundred thousand or more.

The phone, in safe-mode, by default, is what saves the city.

The woman is one of the people who looked up.

---

On the eleventh of August she stops at a stall in the night market near her apartment, two blocks from the canal, and buys soup. The vendor is making pho. The vendor's pho has a particular smell — star anise, cinnamon bark, a touch of caramelized ginger, the broth heavy with charred onion. The woman has eaten this pho before. She has eaten it perhaps thirty times. She likes it.

She likes it tonight too.

The scent enters her in the way scent enters anyone — through the nose, into the olfactory bulb, into the amygdala — and is encoded as one of perhaps four hundred sensory inputs her brain catalogues that evening. She does not register it as significant. Why would she. The scent is the scent of pho.

But the scent is also, because the vendor uses a particular brand of pre-mixed broth base, and that brand has been on the market for eleven years, and that brand is now in the Restricted Stimulus Registry though it will not be entered into the Registry until 2076 — the scent is also the second component of the bound pattern. The first component was the image on the platform screen. The second component is the broth.

She walks home with her soup. She watches television. She sleeps.

She is now Twice-Marked. Two of three. The third, when it arrives, will trigger the cascade.

---

On the morning of August 17 she wakes with a feeling she cannot name. She is not sick. She is not anxious. She is just *off*. The day has the quality of a day that one is observing rather than living, as if she has watched it before in a different version of herself. She goes to work. She does not eat much at lunch. She comes home at six and lies down on the couch and falls asleep there until eight.

She wakes from a dream she does not remember. She is humming.

She does not notice that she is humming for an hour. Her partner notices first.

"What's that?" he says, from the kitchen.

"What's what?"

"That tune. What are you humming?"

She stops. She thinks. She does not know.

"I don't know," she says. "I think I made it up?"

"It's catchy."

He hums it back to her. He has caught the melody from her and is humming it now. She listens.

"That's mine?" she says. "Did I just make that up?"

"You've been humming it for like an hour."

She has not been humming it for an hour. She has been humming it, the case file will determine, for closer to two hours, and the encoded pattern is the third component, and the binding will complete tonight, during sleep, during the consolidation cycle that begins around three a.m. — and she will not wake from that consolidation, and the cascade will fire at three-eighteen, and her partner will find her at six when he wakes for work, and he will scream, and the neighbors will come, and the medics will come, and the cause of death will be filed initially as cardiac arrhythmia in a thirty-two-year-old woman with no prior cardiac history.

That filing will be revised, eleven days later, when the connection is made.

By that point an estimated nineteen thousand people will have completed binding from the August 4 platform exposure, in combination with the various second and third components scattered through the city's August — the pho broth, the tone in the emergency broadcast on August 12, an ambient hum that played in three department stores throughout the week, a perfume launched on August 9, the chorus of a popular song that climbed the charts that week.

Approximately fourteen thousand of those will die in the six months that follow.

She is the first.

She is unidentified in the case file because she had been carrying her ID in a wallet on the kitchen counter and her partner, in his shock, had forgotten where it was, and by the time the medics asked for it her name had become administratively complicated. The case file goes out with her face redacted in the post-mortem photo, and her name listed as *To Be Determined*, and by the time her name is determined, three days later, the case file has been sealed and the redaction has been baked into the system, and she is unidentified forever after, in this file, in this archive.

Her partner will know who she was. Her mother will know who she was. The vendor who made the pho will not know that she was anyone, because the vendor will not know any of this happened, because the connection between the broth and the deaths will not be made publicly for another year.

She was thirty-two. She was the head of the procurement team at a logistics firm in Sankt-Anne. She had a partner she had been with for four years and a cat named Pavel and a brother in Bucharest she texted every Sunday. She liked pho. She liked sunrise photographs of the Adriatic. She had a tune in her head when she died and she did not know where she had learned it.
