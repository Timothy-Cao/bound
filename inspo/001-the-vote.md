> *From the chronicle of the AI Labor Ban hearings, public proceedings.*
> *April 14, 2031. Washington.*

---

There was a woman with a sign across the street from the Senate offices, and Karl Olesen could see her face from the window of room 412 if he leaned slightly. She had been there every morning for three weeks. Her sign said WE WANT TO WORK. Karl had given her face the structure of a name, the way young men did with strangers they wanted to keep believing in. He had decided she was a school administrator who had been pushed out of her job by a procurement-AI vendor in 2028 and had spent the last three years learning to be angry in public.

He did not know if any of this was true. He knew she was the reason he had taken the job.

He drank the coffee from the staffer pool and tried not to read the news on his phone. The news was bad in a way that was familiar to him by now — markets jittering, three more retail chains announcing closures, an op-ed from a man he had not heard of recommending that the Senate be patient. Karl had practiced not being patient. It was the central discipline of his career so far, three months in.

The vote was scheduled for two-thirty. He was supposed to deliver the brief at one. Senator Vail had asked for him by name, which made him feel obscene and twenty-five at once.

"Karl."

It was Davis, who had hired him. Davis was at the door of 412 with his coat over one arm and a manila folder in his other hand. Davis looked like he had not slept in a week, which was correct.

"They're going to ask about the audit framework," Davis said. He set the folder down on the table between them. "Not the labor stuff. Vail wants you to walk them through how the auditing apparatus would work in practice. The number of inspectors. The certification path. The cost-recovery model."

"That's not what I prepared."

"You prepared the right thing. They want a different right thing." Davis's mouth did something that wasn't quite a smile. "It's the auditors who get the labor groups to vote yes. They want to know how many of their people we're putting to work."

Karl looked down at the folder. He understood what Davis was telling him, which was that the most important thing about the Ban was that it required hundreds of thousands of human auditors. The Ban was not just refusing AI. The Ban was building, in the same gesture, a federal-state-private apparatus of compliance officers and oversight technicians and certification reviewers and inspectors, an industry that had not existed last year and would by next year be the third-largest employer in the country. He had not prepared for this because he had thought the auditors were a footnote.

"How many?" he said.

"They want a number in the millions. Tell them three million by year five and they'll vote yes. Tell them less and Vail loses Hoxha and Reyes and the IBEW. We need Hoxha and Reyes and the IBEW."

"Three million is —"

"Don't say it. Just take the brief and write the new one. You have an hour."

Karl took the folder. He understood that he was being asked to write a number he had not modeled, and that the number would appear in the Congressional Record by tonight, and that the number would become real by virtue of its appearance. He understood all of this clearly. He understood it the way young men understand things they will later forget they understood.

He thought of the woman with the sign.

"Three million by year five," he said. "I can do that."

Davis nodded once and left. Karl sat down at the table and opened the folder and began to write.

---

He gave the brief at one o'clock. The number was three million by year five, scaled to eight million by year fifteen. He did not say *scaled to eight million by year fifteen* — he said *with steady-state expansion in the eight-to-ten million range over the medium term* — and the senators wrote this down. Hoxha smiled, briefly. Reyes asked one technical question about cost-recovery, which Karl answered cleanly, and then she sat back and did not ask anything else.

At one-forty Karl was excused. He sat in the antechamber and watched a senior staffer eat a sandwich. He drank water. He did not look at his phone.

At two-fifteen Vail opened debate. At two-twenty-eight Senator Krieger gave the speech everyone had expected him to give about the centrality of human labor to human flourishing, a speech that had been written by his nephew, who had majored in philosophy. The vote was called at two-thirty-one. The Ban passed by a margin of fourteen.

Karl was in the gallery when it passed. He cried, briefly, in a way he hoped no one would notice. A woman two rows over noticed and patted his shoulder and said, "We did good," and Karl wanted to say *I'm staff, I shouldn't be in the gallery,* but instead he said *we did good* back, and the woman went on watching the floor as if she had said it to a son.

---

Outside, on the steps, the protesters had become celebrants. Most of them were holding the same signs but with the language past-tensed in marker — *WE GOT TO WORK* — and Karl looked for the woman from the window and did not find her. He thought maybe she had gone home, or maybe she had been one of the people who had moved on to a different cause earlier in the week, or maybe she had never existed in the way he had needed her to.

Davis was waiting at the curb with two other staffers and a black sedan. Davis had taken his coat off. He looked like a man who would sleep tonight.

"Big day," Davis said. "Drink?"

"Yes."

They went to a place on K Street that did not card anyone who came in with a Senate badge. Karl drank three Old Fashioneds in succession and listened to Davis explain what the next six months would look like — the rulemaking, the audit-staffing schedule, the certification standards. Davis kept saying *what we built today* and Karl kept agreeing.

He thought about the woman in the gallery. *We did good.*

He thought about the senator's nephew who had majored in philosophy.

He thought, distantly, about the model he had written in an hour: three million by year five, eight million by year fifteen. He thought about the model the way one thinks about a poem one has memorized and has not yet decided whether one believes.

It was a Tuesday in April. The Ban had passed. Karl Olesen was twenty-five. He went home around eleven, alone, and slept badly, and woke at four with a clear feeling that something was wrong somewhere he could not point to, and could not get back to sleep, and finally got up at five and made coffee and read the morning news on his phone and felt better when he saw that the Ban was the lead story in every paper.

He did not remember, when he was old, what had woken him at four. He remembered that the Ban had passed. He remembered the woman who had patted his shoulder. He remembered the three Old Fashioneds. He did not remember the four o'clock feeling, and that was perhaps the most important thing about the day, though he would not have called it that at the time and would not have called it that fifty years later either.

He went to work at seven and was at his desk by seven-thirty and stayed until ten.
