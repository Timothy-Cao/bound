> *The document M. read on the morning of her death.*
> *The Office of Memetic Containment has declined to release the original.*
> *The pages that follow are reconstructed from her biometric record*
> *and a partial recovery of her workstation cache.*

---

She had found the document on Monday morning. It had taken her seven hours of searching distributed-archive databases and twelve minutes inside a workstation she was not supposed to have access to, but Bahn — without ever quite agreeing to help her, without ever saying *yes* — had not changed his consortium credentials when he retired, and Marina had been his patient for nine years, and the credentials had let her into the regulatory archive where the 2067 Working Group's papers had been moved when they had been declassified-in-part in 2089. Most of the papers had been declassified in 2089. The one she wanted had not been. It had been moved to the open archive in a procedural error, and someone had caught the error, and the document had been re-restricted, and the re-restriction had not yet been propagated to the access tokens of retired physicians who used to do certain kinds of work.

She had downloaded it. She had read the cover sheet. She had recognized the four signatures.

She had sat at her workstation for twenty minutes without opening the body of the memo.

She had then, with the careful methodical certainty of a person doing what they had decided to do, made coffee, and called Esin, and told Esin where she was, and what she had found, and that she was about to read it. She had asked Esin to call the Office of Memetic Containment if she did not call back within an hour. She had given Esin the file name and the access path. She had said: *I want someone to know.*

Esin had said: *Marina, don't. Please.*

Marina had said: *Esin, I have to.*

Esin had said: *call me back.*

Marina had said: *I will.*

She had put down the phone. She had drunk a sip of the coffee. The coffee had been good. She had thought, distantly, that she was glad the coffee had been good.

She had opened the body of the memo.

---

The body began the way Working Group memoranda always began — *This memorandum represents the consensus recommendation of the Working Group on Anomalous Therapy Response* — and continued through the standard structure: findings, recommendation, signatures. The findings were heavily redacted in places, less so in others. The mortality estimate had not been redacted. The recommendation paragraph had not been redacted. She read it twice.

She read it the way she read every technical document. She read it for the *thing it was telling her,* not the thing it was hiding.

What it was telling her was this: in 2067, the Working Group had known. They had known the phenomenon was real. They had known the mortality rate. They had known the potential for adversarial deployment. They had concluded that classification was, on balance, the action most consistent with the Office's responsibility to public safety.

They had been wrong.

She had known they had been wrong for six days now, in the abstract. Looking at the four signatures she understood it not in the abstract.

She turned the page.

The page after the signatures was an attached handwritten note. The handwriting was sloped, slightly archaic, the kind of cursive a man of a certain age and education had been taught in school. The signature at the bottom was *H.V.*

She read the note.

She read it twice.

The note said:

> *To the Director — I sign the memo as written. My reservation is recorded here and not in the body of the report. I do not believe that classification will hold for long. The pattern is in the air now. The pattern is in the [████]. The pattern will reach the public soon and we will have wasted the chance to give it the names it deserves before someone else gives it the names we will regret.*
>
> *The language must not be spoken in the street.*
>
> *— H.V.*

She read the last line a third time.

*The language must not be spoken in the street.*

She had read this phrase before.

She had not read it in a document. She had not read it in a memo. She had read it in a pamphlet, in 2076 — a Vellum pamphlet, one of the early ones, the kind that had been passed around the consortium that year like a virus and that everyone had read once and then pretended they had not read. The pamphlet had been called, she remembered now, *Concordance.* The phrase she had just read had been on its first page.

Henrik Voss had written the phrase in 2067. The Vellum had been writing the phrase in 2076. The phrase had not been the Vellum's. The phrase had been Voss's. The Vellum had quoted *him.*

She was now sitting in a chair in a room she had broken into, holding a document that proved that one of the four men who had classified the mechanism had been writing, nine years before the cult that would later define the world's theological response to the mechanism, the phrase the cult would later take as its motto.

She thought —

She thought —

She did not think anything for some seconds.

She thought: *they did not just classify. One of them was on their side. One of them classified because he believed it was holy.*

She thought: *this is the foundation. This is what they buried in 1967. This is what they buried in 1067. This is what is —*

She lost the century for a moment. She looked at her hands. The hands were forty years old. The hands were Marina Idris's hands. She remembered her name.

She thought: *I am too certain. I am being too certain. The memory I am about to make is going to be smoothed.*

She thought: *Esin.*

She picked up the phone to call Esin.

She did not get to the call.

The page in her hand had been carefully reproduced from a high-resolution scan of the 2067 original, and the original had been written on a particular kind of typewriter, and the typewriter's spacing had a particular geometry, and the geometry was —

The geometry was —

She did not get to the call.
