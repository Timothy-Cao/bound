> *M. — voicemail to E. Olamide, professional colleague.*
> *Reconstructed from receiving party's archive. April 9, 2080.*

---

Marina opened the kitchen window for the first time in four days. The air outside had been ranked Class C for olfactory density since Tuesday — orange tag — but the wind had turned and someone three floors down was cooking with bay leaves, and she stood at the open window and let the smell come in like a body laying itself across her chest, briefly, and then withdrawing.

She had been clean for nine years.

She did not, when she said this to herself, mean clean in the way a recovering alcoholic meant clean, although the resemblance was something she had been turning over in her mind for several days now. She meant the technical meaning. She was Class Zero. She had a passport that said so. She had the records to prove it — nine years of certified scent, nine years of audited air, nine years of cleared media, nine years of the careful disciplined absence of risk she had built around herself after the bad month in 2071, when she had been Sealed for almost a week before Bahn had figured out what was happening and put her on the protocol that had, against significant odds, brought her back down.

She did not think about the bad month often. The bad month had its own shelf in her interior life and she rarely opened the shelf. But this morning, with the bay-leaf air coming in the window, she thought about the bad month, briefly, because the bay-leaf air felt familiar in a way she could not place, and she wanted to be sure that what she was feeling was *bay leaf* and not something else.

It was bay leaf.

She closed the window.

She made coffee and stood at the counter drinking it and thought, with the precision she brought to most of her thinking, about the meeting.

---

The meeting had been four days ago. The contract had come in through the legitimate channels — a referral from a senior colleague at the consortium, vetted through the standard licensure check, with a budget that was high but not absurd and a client name that meant nothing to her but vetted clean on the registry. The work was, on paper, defensive: she was being asked to design a *signature olfactory profile* for a private high-security residence, which was the kind of work Class A-7 chemists got asked to do roughly twice a year. The Estate work, the industry called it, after the 2079 incident.

Except the parameters had been wrong.

The parameters had specified a profile with a binding-grade complexity that no legitimate residential application required. The molecular structure they wanted was the kind of structure you built when you wanted a scent that would *encode* in a specific way, against a specific population, with a specific intended pairing.

She had recognized it immediately. She had not, immediately, said anything.

She had asked, instead, three clarifying questions, each of which the client representative had answered cleanly and patiently. The client had nothing to hide. The client was, she understood, presenting her with a contract that was either (a) a thoroughly innocent request from a wealthy paranoiac who had hired the wrong consultant to write the spec, or (b) a thoroughly guilty request from someone who had decided that Marina Idris was the chemist most likely, given her temperament, to take the work without asking the questions she was now asking.

She had thought about it for two minutes. The client representative had waited politely.

She had said: *I'm sorry, this isn't something I can take on.*

The client representative had not flinched. The client representative had nodded once and said *of course, we appreciate your time, we'll find someone else,* and they had ended the call cordially, and Marina had sat at her desk for fifteen minutes afterward not doing anything, and then she had gone for a walk, and then she had come home, and that had been the end of it.

She had not called the Office of Memetic Containment.

She had thought about calling. She had decided not to call because what she had refused was — strictly — a residential olfactory commission. The parameters had been wrong, but parameters were not evidence. A senior chemist could have written that spec. A wealthy client could have, in good faith, paid for it. She had refused on instinct, and instinct was not a thing one filed reports about.

She had also decided, in the back of her mind in a place she did not look at directly, that calling the OMC would put her on a list, and that the list would put her in conversations she did not want to have, and that the conversations would slow her career for a year and possibly forever.

She had reasons. The reasons had been, on the morning of the refusal, sufficient.

By the morning of the bay-leaf air, four days later, the reasons were less sufficient.

---

She called Esin at eleven.

Esin was not at her desk. Esin was almost never at her desk. Marina got the voicemail prompt and waited the beat she always waited for and then she said —

*Esin, hi. It's Marina. Listen — I want to bounce something off you. I had a referral come through last week, from the consortium, for a residential olfactory commission, and the parameters were — well. They were unusual. I turned it down. But I've been thinking about it for a few days now and I'm wondering if I should have, you know, kicked it upstairs. I keep telling myself it's nothing. I keep —*

*Anyway. Call me. I'm at home today. I'd like to talk.*

She ended the call. She put the phone down. She stood at the counter for another minute. She thought about going for a walk. She decided to work.

---

She did not get to work for very long.

At three-eighteen in the afternoon a delivery man buzzed her apartment. He had a package addressed to her. The package was a small wooden case, lacquered, of the kind that perfume samples sometimes shipped in. There was no return address.

She brought it inside. She set it on the counter. She looked at it.

It was the kind of package she would, in the normal course of her work, have opened without thinking.

She did not open it.

She put on her gloves. She put on a scent-mask. She went to the building's basement, where there was a sealed-environment chamber the residents' association maintained for exactly this kind of situation, and she carried the package down in a sealed transport bag, and she put it in the chamber, and she set the chamber's analytical sensors to log the contents passively without releasing the seal, and she went back upstairs.

She washed her hands. She washed her hands again.

She sat down at her desk.

She thought, for the first time clearly, about the bad month in 2071. About the moment, on the fourth day of the bad month, when she had realized she could not remember whether she had been wearing her audio scrubbers on the morning of the seventeenth. She had been *certain* she had been. She had been certain in the particular way she had since learned not to trust — the smoothed certainty, the certainty that was too clean.

She was, this afternoon, *certain* she had refused the contract.

She was certain in a way she did not, anymore, trust.

She picked up the phone. She did not call Esin again. She called the OMC.

The OMC put her on hold for thirty-two minutes.

When the duty officer came on the line she said *my name is Marina Idris, I am a Class A-7 licensed chemist, and I would like to file a report,* and the duty officer said *one moment please,* and put her on hold for another nine minutes, and Marina sat with the phone against her ear and watched the bay-leaf wind move the curtain at the kitchen window and thought, distantly, *the third thing kills.*

She did not know why she had thought it.

When the duty officer came back, Marina was no longer entirely sure what she had wanted to report.

She said *I'm sorry, I think I called by mistake,* and hung up.

The bay-leaf air continued to come in the window. The lacquered box sat in the chamber in the basement, its sensors logging. Esin did not call back until evening.

By then, Marina had begun to investigate herself.
