# Daily Life in 2080

## Sensory environment

- Phones load images as text descriptions first; user must explicitly request the visual.
- Public screens are rare, low-detail, and certified.
- Wallpaper is plain. Patterned wallpaper exists in homes (often deliberately, for sensory saturation) but not in public buildings.
- Clothing trends matte and patternless. Stripes, spirals, animated fabrics, reflective jewelry, and depth patterns are restricted in most public venues.
- Optical illusions are taboo in casual settings; viewed as juvenile or hostile.
- Restaurants advertise "scent-neutral dining" and "certified non-binding menu visuals."
- Public bathrooms use scentless soap. Strong perfume in public reads as smoking-indoors did in the 2010s.
- Emergency alerts are plain text with certified tones. Old siren systems were retired in 2073.

## Private suspicions of the classification system

By 2080, most adults have begun privately suspecting they are not actually Class 0. The suspicion arises from common experiences: smelling something at a market that produces an inexplicable feeling of *recognition*, dreaming patterns one cannot place, watching a stranger across the platform freeze briefly at a familiar sound and walk on. Public-health communication continues to insist that the Passport classification is reliable. Private experience increasingly does not.

Some people seek out clinical screening for genuine reassurance (limited utility; the clinical screens catch only registered patterns). Some pay for "deep screening" at underground services (expensive, frequently charlatans). Most live with the suspicion and do not look further. The cost of confirming oneself Marked is too high in most lives: insurance, employment, social standing all hang on the Passport classification.

The result is a population of citizens who are nominally Clean and privately uncertain, going about their work in a state of low-grade dread that the public-health apparatus does not officially recognize.

## What is dangerous to read

The Madeleine Effect (R17) means that books, photographs, films, and even conversation can trigger binding completion in subjects who have encoded components. The Cognitively Active Media regulation extends to:

- **Cookbooks** with detailed scent descriptions (licensed, sometimes pruned, regional editions vary)
- **Food photography** in newspapers and magazines (restricted in many jurisdictions; replaced with text-only food coverage in some)
- **Travel writing** about specific cuisines (requires sensory clearance)
- **Memoirs** that vividly describe perfumes, kitchens, childhood smells (reviewed before publication)
- **Children's books** are heavily pruned of evocative scent description (the licensed editions are notably less sensory than pre-2070 children's literature)
- **Restaurant menus** in some jurisdictions cannot include taste descriptors. Servers are forbidden to verbally describe dishes
- **Old novels** with rich sensory prose have been retroactively reviewed; some are now restricted to certified readers only

Most readers learn to skim sensory passages instinctively. Some find this skimming itself a small daily grief. Many of the most beloved novels of the pre-2070 era are functionally inaccessible to anyone who has been formally classified Marked or Twice-Marked.

## Etiquette

- **"Safe to view?"** before showing any image. Universal. A child learns this around age four.
- **Announce before removing AR goggles** in a shared space, even briefly. Removing without announcement reads as aggressive.
- **Don't hum unknown tunes** in public. Whistling a tune nobody recognizes triggers visible discomfort.
- **Don't send unsolicited media.** Sending an unannounced image to someone's phone is the modern equivalent of physical intimidation.
- **Public scent use** is restricted by ordinance in most cities. Wearing perfume on a train is a misdemeanor.
- **"Look at this"** has become rude. "Cleared if you want it" is the polite form.

## Children's safety training

Taught from kindergarten, often as a rhyme:

> Eyes down.
> Ears covered.
> Breath held.

Reinforced by:
- Don't copy strange drawings.
- Don't repeat unfamiliar rhythms.
- Don't describe dangerous images (describing can encode them in the listener).
- Report repeated dreams to an adult.
- Report unknown songs stuck in your head.
- Don't smell unknown objects.

Kids who break these rules are not punished, they're tested. A child who hums something unknown for three days gets pulled in for an exposure screening.

## Major life events

- **Weddings**: require safe music, safe flowers, cleared photo displays. The bride's bouquet is often plain greenery; flower scent at scale is too risky for a crowd.
- **Funerals**: incense, music, and slideshows require clearance. Many families forgo all three; quiet funerals are now the norm.
- **Concerts**: require sensory clearance. Big tours hire full clearance teams. Underground / unclearance shows are a major youth subculture.
- **Movie theaters**: rare. The ones that remain are controlled environments, small audiences, registered patrons, cleared media only. Repertory cinema is dead; old films can't be re-certified at reasonable cost.
- **Museums**: require waivers and filtered viewing. Old masters viewed through goggles only. The originals are rarely on public display anymore.
- **Dating**: text-first profiles dominate. Sharing a photo is a third-date intimacy. "Want to see me?" is a real question.

## Civic infrastructure

- Public bodycams record in low-fidelity safe mode by default.
- Court evidence cannot be directly displayed if hazardous; testimony and description substitute.
- Police hazard-response is a specialized unit, "Stim Response", distinct from explosives or biohazard.
- Hazard-scanning doorways at airports, courts, hospitals, government buildings.
- Visual-density meters and smell detectors in transit hubs.
- Some neighborhoods have public "stim alarms", sirens triggered by detection of suspect patterns.

## Daily protective habits

Common among ordinary citizens (not paranoid, just careful):

- Goggles on in transit; off at home.
- Standard-issue scent mask in pocket; deployed in crowds.
- Audio scrubber in earbuds during commute.
- One drink before bed (folk consolidation prophylaxis). Younger people sometimes take low-dose melatonin disruptors instead.
- Periodic visits to a stink den, pattern bar, or scent-chaotic market, depending on class and geography, to refresh sensory saturation.

The wealthy do this clinically. The poor do it socially. Most of the middle does some of both and feels vaguely guilty about not doing more.
