# The Collection — Working Format

**Form:** Linked short story collection (mosaic novel).
**Length:** ~120 pages total, ~25,000–35,000 words across ~20 stories of 3–8 pages each.
**Frame:** Archival reconstruction. The book is presented as a body of stories assembled by an unnamed reconstructor from recovered documents, interviews, biometric logs, case files, and personal correspondence. The reconstructor surfaces as a character only in the final story.

This format supersedes the earlier single-POV novella plan ([`the-book.md`](the-book.md)) and is now the working direction. Variations on character and individual stories are expected; the structural shape below is stable.

## Why this form

- **Honors the worldbuilding.** Every named actor, mechanism, defense generation, and cascading effect can have its story. Nothing built is wasted.
- **Reinforces the theme structurally.** "Humanity is more cooperative than the system permits" is a cumulative claim. Twenty testimonies build the argument better than one arc can. The book's *form* is the argument.
- **Solves novel-risk.** Graceful degradation — a weak story can be cut without restructure.
- **Variable register per story.** Some literary, some procedural, some intimate, some horror, some satirical. The variety becomes a feature.
- **The page-of-text climax becomes one story's ending** rather than a structural midpoint — heightens singularity.
- **Time-spanning is natural.** Stories from 2030s to 2080s let the political-historical evolution be visible. Reader watches the trap close generation by generation.

## Comp titles

- *World War Z* (Brooks) — oral history mosaic, closest in tone and method.
- *Cloud Atlas* (Mitchell) — interlocking pieces across history.
- *A Visit from the Goon Squad* (Egan) — formal play, time-spanning.
- *Exhalation* (Ted Chiang) — themed philosophical-SF collection.
- *Olive Kitteridge* (Strout) — linked stories with recurring characters.
- *Salt Slow* (Armfield) / *Her Body and Other Parties* (Machado) — literary-horror register.

## Story opening framing — archival headers

Every story opens with a short framing block (3–6 lines max) presented as the reconstructor's source material. The block varies in *type* by story: case file, leaked memo, audio diary transcript, redacted clinical log, personal correspondence, public record, etc.

The variation is meaningful. Some characters had state files (institutional sources). Some have only personal records (intimate). Some had nothing recoverable. The header *type* tells the reader who could be remembered and how.

### Discipline for headers

- **SHORT.** Three to six lines max. If a header expands into paragraphs, it has become exposition in costume.
- **Real artifacts, not flavor.** "Case file 2069-WMK-08-7724" is a working file number; "FILE: VICTIM TRACE 4.0" is fake-corporate copywriting. Sweat the details.
- **No header explains the story.** Headers orient; the prose tells. A header that gives the plot away has done too much.
- **No header should be the book.** A reader who skips them should still get the whole story. Their absence shouldn't break anything.
- **The "no header" choice is rare and deliberate.** Once or twice in the whole book. The Archivist's piece is the canonical example. Possibly one other.

## Recurring elements across stories

Linked collections live or die on the invisible connective tissue. The following should recur deliberately across stories. To be tracked as the draft develops.

### Characters who appear in multiple stories
- **K. Olesen** (an architect, named in "The Memo" and "The Designer," possibly referenced obliquely in others).
- **A. Ranjit** (Office of Memetic Containment; appears in "The Censor's Hum," may surface in others).
- **L. Han** (eldercare nurse from "First Cardiac Event," possibly older and recurring in "Bound" or elsewhere).
- **Marina** (anchor character — protagonist of three late stories: "Marina's Refusal," "Marina at the Window," "The Document").
- Others to be developed.

### Objects, phrases, images
- The **bay-leaf scent** (recurring olfactory motif — appears in Marina's chapters, possibly in earlier stories as innocent texture).
- The **phrase "I'm too certain"** (Marina's, but possibly echoed by other characters in moments of memory-doubt).
- A specific **public-service poster** or recurring street-corner billboard text that appears in several stories at different decades — same wall, different posters.
- **The melody the teenager hums** — possibly first heard, innocent and unmemotoxic, in an earlier story.

### Structural rhymes
- The **first story** (The Vote) and the **architect's diary** (The Designer) bracket the historical argument. Same person, fifty years apart.
- The **breakthrough scene** in "A Patient Recognizes Her Daughter" and the **terminal scene** in "Bound" rhyme: both eldercare, both about recognition. One is the medical promise; the other is its cost.
- "Marina's Refusal" and "The Document" bracket the chemist's arc.

## Process notes for the writing phase

1. **Start with one story to test the form.** I will write "Marina's Refusal" first as a standalone 5–7 page piece. If the voice lands, the rest follows. If it doesn't, we adjust.
2. **Don't write in order.** Strongest stories first. Weak ideas can be cut without restructuring.
3. **Use the canon as substrate, not exposition.** Most of what we've built will inform the prose without appearing in it.
4. **Honor warmth.** Each story should contain at least one moment of warmth or small joy. The collection's bleakness must be earned against love and humor, not asserted.
5. **Track recurring elements actively.** A simple file mapping which character / object / phrase appears in which story keeps the connective tissue intentional.
