# Scene Seeds and Hooks

A working list of scenes, moments, and situations that feel alive in this world. Not all will be used. Most will be reshaped. Maintained as inspiration and texture.

## Public-life scenes

**The train whistle.** A child whistles an unknown tune on a train. Adults go silent. Someone activates an audio scrubber. The parent is offended. Everyone pretends this is normal. Show: the etiquette in action, the parent's class, the child's obliviousness, the way no one quite makes eye contact.

**Scent-neutral dining.** A restaurant displays the sign: *"Scent-neutral dining. Certified non-binding menu visuals."* A first date. One of them removes their goggles to read the wine list. The other notices.

**Court exhibit 12.** A judge says: "Exhibit 12 exists but cannot be shown, heard, or described in detail. The jury will accept the witness's certified summary." The defense counsel objects, knowing it will be overruled.

**The cleared photo.** Someone asks before sharing an old photo with a new partner: *"It's cleared. Green-tagged yesterday."* Soft, tender, mundane. The kind of intimacy that didn't exist before.

**The contested funeral song.** The family argues over whether they can play the deceased's favorite song. The venue requires clearance. The song was decertified in 2076 for "structural irregularity." The widow doesn't care.

**The bound dementia patient.** An elderly woman in eldercare recognizes her daughter after a sensory treatment. The nurse smiles, takes notes, then quietly flags a possible binding event in the patient's file. The family is not told.

**The dealer's three packages.** A black-market dealer sells three items separately to three different clients: a wallpaper sample, an old ringtone, and a perfume vial. None of the clients know about the others. The dealer might not either.

## Class scenes

**The poor neighborhood.** Cheap AR goggles overblur warning signs, faces, graffiti, traffic lights. A pedestrian doesn't see the truck. The truck driver, in his own cheap goggles, doesn't see the pedestrian. The intersection has the lowest visual-density rating in the district. Nobody can afford to fix it.

**The rich home.** A private residence with custom safe lighting, cleared art, scent-neutral flowers, a neutral room for the host's anxious teenager. The host serves tea, removes their thin elegant goggles to drink, replaces them afterward. The guest, in cheap chunky standard issue, declines tea, can't drink without removing their goggles, doesn't want to.

**The stink den.** Smoky, loud, the smell hits like a wall. A middle-class doctor visits incognito on her doctor's orders, clinical CDP is too expensive this month. The regulars know her face. They don't say anything.

## Mechanism scenes

**The sudden death.** A woman walking through a perfume counter in a department store. She freezes mid-step. Her hand goes to her chest. She drops. The store has a protocol for this, sensors, a designated responder, a screen that drops down. Shoppers walk around the screen. The intercom plays a calming tone. A cleanup crew comes within minutes. Average bystander reaction: mild discomfort, continues shopping.

**The drift.** A young man wakes from a dream he can't shake. The same melody, three nights in a row. He doesn't tell his parents. He doesn't go to the clinic. He's Marked and he doesn't know it. The reader knows.

**The censor's contamination.** A media-clearance technician begins humming a tune at work. Her coworkers notice. By the third day, three of them are humming it. The censor AI she's been working with has been producing subtly hazardous output for weeks. Nobody has caught it yet.

**The dilution party.** A teenager's underground "dilution party", friends gather to deliberately overload all three sensory channels with chaotic variant content. They share a bottle. They light strange candles. They play random tonal sequences. It's half medical, half spiritual, half a way to feel something.

## Plot-shaped hooks

**The leaked classified attack.** Word reaches a journalist that the SSDO has been deploying two-component memotoxins in foreign operations. Confirming this requires accessing classified material that may itself be hazardous.

**The Bound witness.** A Class 4 survivor of a classified attack is in protective custody. She knows what happened. Her memory is evidence, and also potentially a weapon, describing what she saw could encode the pattern in the listener. Her interrogators wear filters. The investigators argue about whether she should be allowed to testify, treated, or quietly euthanized.

**The clinic patient who recovers too well.** An eldercare patient under sensory therapy recovers cognitive function far beyond expectations. The treating physician realizes the AI has discovered something new, and that publishing this discovery would also make it weaponizable.

**The retired bounty hunter.** A former Stim Response officer, now hunting black-market components for an insurance company, finds a smuggling route that doesn't fit any known pattern. The components don't match any registered memotoxin. They're building something new.

**The cult's revelation.** A small cult claims to have received a complete memotoxin pattern in a shared dream. They are obviously delusional, except their members keep dying.

**The art-restoration job.** An archivist is hired to restore a pre-regulation painting from a private collection. Halfway through, she realizes the painting contains a known hazardous geometry. She doesn't know if the collector knows. She doesn't know if she's been encoding it for weeks.

## Tone notes

- Day-to-day mood is *cautious normal*, not *gothic horror*. People shop, joke, fall in love. The threat is ambient.
- When horror surfaces, it surfaces *socially*, through etiquette failure, through the wrong question asked, through the wrong song hummed.
- Sudden deaths in public are common enough to be procedural, rare enough to be unsettling. Show them being routine. The reader's discomfort is the point.
- Class differences are mostly visual: goggle quality, scent neutrality, who can afford to remove their filter at home.
