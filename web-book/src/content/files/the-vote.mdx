There was a woman with a sign across the street from the Senate offices. Karl Olesen could see her face from room 412 if he leaned a little. She'd been there every morning for three weeks. Her sign said WE WANT TO WORK.

He'd given her a story. He had decided she was a school administrator, displaced in 2028 by some procurement-AI vendor, and that she'd spent the last three years learning to be angry in public. He didn't know if any of this was true. He knew she was the reason he'd taken the job.

The vote was at two-thirty. He was supposed to deliver the brief at one. Senator Vail had asked for him by name, which made him feel obscene and twenty-five at once.

"Karl."

Davis was at the door of 412 with his coat over one arm and a manila folder in his other hand. Davis looked like he hadn't slept in a week, which was correct.

"They're going to ask about the audit framework," Davis said. He set the folder on the table. "Not the labor stuff. Vail wants you to walk them through how the auditing apparatus would work in practice. The number of inspectors. The certification path. The cost-recovery model."

"That isn't what I prepared."

"You prepared the right thing. They want a different right thing." Davis's mouth did something that wasn't a smile. "It's the auditors who get the labor groups to vote yes. They want to know how many of their people we're putting to work."

Karl looked at the folder. He understood. The most important thing about the Ban wasn't that it refused AI. The most important thing about the Ban was that it required hundreds of thousands of human auditors, certification reviewers, compliance officers. The Ban wasn't a refusal. The Ban was a hiring program. He hadn't prepared for this because he'd thought the auditors were a footnote.

"How many," he said.

"They want a number in the millions. Three million by year five, you get Hoxha and Reyes and the IBEW. Less, you lose them. We need Hoxha and Reyes and the IBEW."

"Three million is."

"Don't say it. Take the brief and write the new one. You have an hour."

Karl took the folder. He understood he was being asked to write a number he had not modeled, that the number would appear in the Congressional Record by tonight, and that the number would become real by virtue of its appearance. He understood all of this clearly. He understood it the way young men understand things they will later forget they understood.

He thought of the woman with the sign.

"Three million by year five," he said. "I can do that."

Davis nodded once and left. Karl sat down at the table and opened the folder and began to write.

***

He gave the brief at one o'clock. The number was three million by year five, scaling to eight by year fifteen. He didn't say *scaling to eight*. He said *with steady-state expansion in the eight-to-ten million range over the medium term*, and the senators wrote this down. Hoxha smiled, briefly. Reyes asked one technical question about cost-recovery, which Karl answered cleanly. Then she sat back and asked nothing else.

He was excused at one-forty. He sat in the antechamber and watched a senior staffer eat a sandwich. He drank water. He didn't look at his phone.

At two-fifteen Vail opened debate. At two-twenty-eight Senator Krieger gave the speech everyone had expected him to give about the centrality of human labor to human flourishing, a speech that had been written by his nephew, who had majored in philosophy. The vote was called at two-thirty-one. The Ban passed by a margin of fourteen.

Karl was in the gallery when it passed. He cried, briefly, in a way he hoped no one would notice. A woman two rows over noticed and patted his shoulder. *We did good,* she said. Karl wanted to say *I'm staff, I shouldn't be in the gallery*. He said *we did good* back. The woman went on watching the floor as if she had said it to a son.

***

Outside, on the steps, the protesters had become celebrants. Most of them held the same signs but with the language past-tensed in marker. WE GOT TO WORK. Karl looked for the woman from the window and didn't find her. He thought maybe she'd gone home. He thought maybe she'd been someone else by now, on to a different cause. He thought maybe she'd never existed in the way he'd needed her to.

Davis was at the curb with two other staffers and a black sedan. Davis had taken his coat off. He looked like a man who would sleep tonight.

"Big day," Davis said. "Drink?"

"Yes."

They went to a place on K Street that didn't card anyone with a Senate badge. Karl drank three Old Fashioneds in succession and listened to Davis explain what the next six months would look like. The rulemaking. The audit-staffing schedule. The certification standards. Davis kept saying *what we built today* and Karl kept agreeing.

He thought about the woman in the gallery. *We did good.*

He thought about the senator's nephew who had majored in philosophy.

He thought, distantly, about the model he had written in an hour. Three million by year five. Eight by year fifteen. He thought about the model the way one thinks about a poem one has memorized and has not yet decided whether one believes.

It was a Tuesday in April. The Ban had passed. Karl Olesen was twenty-five. He went home around eleven, alone, and slept badly, and woke at four with the clear feeling that something was wrong somewhere he couldn't point to. He couldn't get back to sleep. He got up at five and made coffee and read the morning news on his phone, and he felt better when he saw that the Ban was the lead story in every paper.

He didn't remember, when he was old, what had woken him at four. He remembered that the Ban had passed. He remembered the woman who had patted his shoulder. He remembered the three Old Fashioneds. He didn't remember the four o'clock feeling, and that was perhaps the most important thing about the day, though he wouldn't have called it that at the time and wouldn't have called it that fifty years later either.

He went to work at seven and was at his desk by seven-thirty and stayed until ten.
