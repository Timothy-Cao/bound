She had found the document on Monday morning.

It had taken her seven hours of searching distributed-archive databases and twelve minutes inside a workstation she was not supposed to have access to. Bahn, without ever quite agreeing to help her, without ever saying *yes*, had not changed his consortium credentials when he retired, and Marina had been his patient for nine years, and the credentials had let her into the regulatory archive where the 2067 Working Group's papers had been moved when they had been declassified in part in 2089. The one she wanted had not been declassified. It had been moved to the open archive in a procedural error. Someone had caught the error. The document had been re-restricted. The re-restriction had not propagated to the access tokens of retired physicians who used to do certain kinds of work.

She had downloaded it.

She had read the cover sheet.

She had recognized the four signatures.

She had sat at the workstation for twenty minutes without opening the body of the memo.

She had then, with the careful methodical certainty of a person doing what she had decided to do, made coffee, and called Esin, and told Esin where she was, and what she had found, and that she was about to read it.

She had asked Esin to call the Office of Memetic Containment if she did not call back within an hour. She had given Esin the file name and the access path.

She had said, *I want someone to know.*

Esin had said, *Marina, don't. Please.*

She had said, *Esin, I have to.*

Esin had said, *call me back.*

She had said, *I will.*

She had put the phone down. She had drunk a sip of the coffee. The coffee was good. She had thought, distantly, that she was glad the coffee was good.

She opened the body of the memo.

She read it for the thing it was telling her, not the thing it was hiding.

What it was telling her was: in 2067 the Working Group had known. They had known the phenomenon was real. They had known the mortality rate. They had known the potential for adversarial deployment. They had concluded that classification was, on balance, the action most consistent with the Office's responsibility to public safety.

They had been wrong.

She had known they had been wrong for six days, in the abstract. Looking at the four signatures she understood it in something other than the abstract.

She turned the page.

The page after the signatures was a handwritten note. The handwriting was sloped, slightly archaic, the kind of cursive a man of a certain age and education had been taught in school. The signature at the bottom was *H.V.*

She read the note.

She read it twice.

The last line said: *The language must not be spoken in the street.*

She had read this phrase before.

She had not read it in a document. She had read it in a pamphlet, in 2076. A Vellum pamphlet, one of the early ones, that had been passed around the consortium that year the way certain kinds of pages get passed in a year when no one is in charge. The pamphlet had been called *Concordance*. The phrase had been on its first page.

Henrik Voss had written the phrase in 2067.

The Vellum had written the phrase in 2076.

The phrase had not been the Vellum's. The phrase had been Voss's. The Vellum had quoted him.

She was now sitting in a chair in a room she had broken into holding a document that proved one of the four men who classified the mechanism had been writing, nine years before the cult that would define the world's theological response to the mechanism, the phrase the cult would later take as its motto.

She thought.

She thought.

She did not think anything for a few seconds.

She thought: *they did not just classify. One of them was on their side. One of them classified because he believed it was holy.*

She thought: *this is the foundation. This is what they buried in 2067.*

She lost the century for a moment. She looked at her hands. The hands were forty years old. The hands were Marina Idris's hands. She remembered her name.

She thought: *I am too certain. I am being too certain. The memory I am about to make is going to be smoothed.*

She thought: *Esin.*

She picked up the phone to call Esin.

She did not get to the call.

The page in her hand had been carefully reproduced from a high-resolution scan of the 2067 original. The original had been written on a particular kind of typewriter. The typewriter's spacing had a particular geometry. The geometry was.

The geometry was.

She did not get to the call.
