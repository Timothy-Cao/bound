Catherine Reyes had voted for the Ban in 2031, and on the seventeenth day of the hearings she sat in the gallery and watched Karl Olesen, older now, calmer, mid-career, argue for cracking it open.

She had been in this gallery before. Different room, same building. Different vote. She'd been forty-eight the first time, and she'd cried then too, and the IBEW staffer next to her had patted her shoulder and said *we did good*, and Catherine had believed him. She was sixty-seven now. The staffer was dead. The shoulder was hers.

Olesen was forty-four. He had the kind of face that wore middle age well. Gray at the temples. A small line at the corner of his mouth that suggested he had recently learned to be patient. He spoke without notes. He spoke for thirty-five minutes, and at no point did he say the word *Ban*. He said *the framework of 2031*. He said *the protective architecture our predecessors built*. He said *our duty to that architecture is to evolve it where the evidence demands*.

He was good. She watched him be good. She had been good once and remembered the feeling.

The witnesses before him had been doctors. A geriatrician from Lyon who had read a list. Names of her patients. The years of their decline. The months between their lucid moments and their deaths. A neurologist from Geneva who had brought slides. A nurse from a public hospital outside Manchester who had said *I cannot work this job another year* and had cried, on camera, into the microphone. The camera hadn't cut away. The chair of the subcommittee had thanked her. The room had felt heavier afterward in a way Catherine remembered from a long time ago.

Then Olesen had been called.

The chair said *Mr. Olesen, you served as junior counsel during the 2031 hearings, is that correct,* and Olesen said *I did, Madam Chair*, and the chair said *would you tell us how the events you helped frame in 2031 should inform what we do now,* and Olesen had begun to speak.

Catherine listened. She listened the way she'd once trained herself to listen to opposition counsel. For the small slip. The mis-emphasis. The place where the speaker overcorrects. She didn't find one. Olesen had been preparing this testimony, she understood, for fifteen years. Fifteen years of practicing not being patient about a different thing.

He said: *We did not pass the Ban because we were afraid of machines. We passed it because we were afraid of forgetting that our neighbors were human.*

He said: *Today we are being asked to remember the same thing in a different direction. The neighbors we are forgetting are old, and demented, and dying without dignity in a country we promised to be better than.*

He said: *The Exception we are debating is narrow. It is medicine. It is care. It is not the door we closed in 2031. It is a smaller door, opened beside it, for the specific purpose of letting our parents die in peace.*

Catherine had to close her eyes for a moment. She hadn't expected him to be this good.

When she opened them, the camera was on him still, and his hands were folded on the table in front of him, and he was waiting for the next question. He didn't look up at the gallery. He didn't have to. He knew what was in it.

***

She had been Catherine Reyes of the IBEW. Then Catherine Reyes of the Labor-Auditing Council, after the Ban. Then Catherine Reyes of the Workers' Cooperative Authority, after the Council had been spun out of the Department of Commerce in 2039. Then nothing, after 2044, when she had let her credentials lapse because she had begun to lose track of which committees she was on. She had been good then too. She had been the woman who got the deal done, who counted the votes, who said *we need Hoxha* and was correct.

She had voted for the Ban because she'd believed, in 2031, that what they were buying was a permanent shape. A society that had refused the obvious cheap solution to the labor problem and would now have to invent harder, better, more human solutions. The Ban had been the no. The yes had been everything that came after.

But what had come after was, mostly, eight million auditors. The harder solutions hadn't come. Or they'd come slowly, and most of them had been undone by the demographics, and most of the rest had been undone by the pandemic, and what was left in 2050 was not a better society than 1995. It was a slower one. With fewer children. With more anxious adults. With a generation of cognitively-impaired survivors who hadn't been there in 2031 and wouldn't have voted the way Catherine had voted.

She knew this. She'd known this for about a decade. She hadn't known what to do about it.

The Exception was not what she would have voted for. But she had counted the votes. Old habit. She knew where it would land. Hoxha was dead. Reyes, herself, no longer had a vote. The new IBEW had already endorsed the Exception. The pro-Ban hardliners in the gallery were three rows behind her. A couple in their seventies. A young man in a blazer she didn't recognize. They would lose. Olesen would win.

She watched him a while longer. He answered three more questions. He was excused at four-twelve. He stood up, careful with his back, she noticed, a man older than his face, and gathered his folder and walked out without looking up.

She watched him go.

***

In the hallway afterward, she saw him again. He was at the elevator. There was a woman with him she didn't know, a younger staffer. They weren't talking. He was reading something on his tablet.

Catherine considered, briefly, going up to him. She had something to say and didn't know what it was. *Do you remember me*. No. *You don't know me but I voted yes in 2031*. Possibly. *I'm sorry*. For what. She was the one who had voted to build the wall he was now politely dismantling. He had been twenty-five then. She had been forty-eight. The breach was her fault, not his.

She didn't go up to him. The elevator opened. He stepped in. The doors closed.

Catherine walked out into the September afternoon and stood on the steps for a while and watched the city be a city. There were no protesters today. The Ban had been a generation ago. The Exception was being debated by people who had been children when the IBEW had marched on the Senate. There was no sign to hold. There was no slogan ready. The people who would lose this vote had already lost.

She thought about the geriatrician's list. She thought about her own father, who had died in 2044 in a state-run home outside Madrid, alone, in a bed that had not been changed for forty hours, with a phone in his hand he could no longer use.

She walked home. The vote was three days away. She didn't need to be in the gallery for it.

The Exception passed on the twenty-sixth, by a margin of nine.

The young man in the blazer wrote, later that year, the first essay in what would become the Restorer pamphlet tradition. He used Catherine's name in the dedication. She did not read it.

Karl Olesen, that night, drank one whiskey alone in his hotel and slept eight hours and did not wake at four.
