The patient's name was Marta. Lihua Han wrote *M.* in the official report because the protocol called for initials in the body and the full name only on the cover sheet. In her notes, the notes she kept in the small leather book she'd bought in 2061 and would still be using when she retired, she wrote *Marta*.

She'd known Marta for two years. Marta had been a music teacher in Krakow before her diagnosis. Marta had liked the sleep-therapy chamber because she said it sounded like the sea, although the sleep-therapy chamber didn't sound like the sea, because the sleep-therapy chamber didn't really sound like anything at all. The audio was below the threshold of conscious hearing. What Marta had heard, when she'd said *it sounds like the sea*, was something the machine had played for her brain and not for her ears.

Marta had died at four-eighteen in the morning of November ninth. The cardiac alarm had pulled Lihua from her station at four-nineteen. She'd been at the bedside by four-twenty. The patient had been in cardiac arrhythmia for, the chart said, eleven seconds before flatline. The therapy program had been running for forty-two minutes. The visual was a low-luminance algorithmic pattern. The audio was the sub-threshold sleep-induction sequence the team had been refining for three months. The scent was the patient's home-comfort blend, which her daughter had brought from Krakow in a small glass bottle.

Lihua did the things she'd been trained to do. She started compressions. She called the response team. She watched the patient's daughter cry, in the corner of the bay, with one hand over her mouth. The response team arrived at four-twenty-four. Resuscitation was attempted for twelve minutes. The patient was declared dead at four-thirty-six.

The therapy machine was still running.

Lihua remembered, later, that the first thing she did after the team had stopped working on Marta was reach over to the console and turn the machine off. The therapy program had been at minute fifty-one. The pattern on the wall had been a kind of slow blue spiral. The machine had said, in a soft voice meant only for the operator, *Therapy session interrupted at minute fifty-one. Resume? Cancel?* Lihua had pressed cancel. The spiral had gone. The bay had been quiet except for the patient's daughter, who was weeping now more openly.

Lihua had said *I'm so sorry* and had taken the daughter to the family room.

The cause of death was filed as cardiac arrhythmia, primary. It was correct, in the sense that Marta's heart had stopped. It was incomplete, in the sense that Lihua knew her patient.

Marta's cardiac workup, two months prior, had been within normal parameters for an eighty-four-year-old. Her heart had been the strongest organ in her body. The dementia had been advancing. The heart had been fine. Cardiologists at the center had remarked on it. The phrase *she's got the heart of a sixty-year-old* had appeared, twice, in her chart.

Lihua wrote her report. She used the words the chart wanted. *Cardiac event during scheduled therapy session, no apparent equipment malfunction, no operator error noted.* She didn't write the words she was thinking, which were *I don't believe this*. She'd been a nurse for sixteen years. She knew what hearts did. Marta's heart had not been ready to stop.

She filed the report at six in the morning, after her shift, sitting at the workstation at the back of the unit while the night cleaners moved through with their carts. She sent the report into the system and waited the four seconds it took to clear. The system said *Received. Reference: SCN-2066-0044.* She wrote that reference number in her leather book under Marta's name. Then she went home and slept for nine hours.

***

The first follow-up came on the thirteenth. It was an email from a supervisor she did not directly report to, asking if she could come in early on Friday for a *quick conversation* about the November ninth incident. The email used the word *incident* in a way Lihua noticed.

She came in early on Friday. The conversation was held in a small room she'd never been in before, on a floor she didn't work on. The supervisor, his name was Hartmann, was kind. He thanked her for her thoroughness. He said the report had been reviewed at the regional level. He said there had been some *follow-up inquiry* into the therapy parameters used during Marta's session, that the inquiry had been concluded, and that the determination had been that no equipment malfunction had occurred and no operator error had been identified.

"That's what I wrote," Lihua said.

"Yes," Hartmann said. "That's exactly what you wrote. We appreciate that."

He paused. He looked at the table between them. He had a folder in front of him that he hadn't opened.

"I wonder," he said, "if I could ask you, going forward, to address any future *unusual events* in your unit through me, rather than the standard report channel. We are. There has been some interest, from the regional office, in standardizing how we handle these things. To keep the protocols clean."

Lihua looked at him. She understood what he was saying.

"Of course," she said.

"You're a very good nurse, Lihua."

"Thank you."

"You'll let me know? If there's another one?"

"Of course."

They shook hands. Lihua went back to her unit and finished her shift.

***

She wrote in her leather book that night: *Hartmann thinks there will be another one.* She underlined *another one* twice.

***

The following week she requested a transfer to the small specialty unit at the back of the building that worked with patients who had failed the standard therapy protocols. The work was harder. The pay was the same. Lihua had a son who would turn six the following March.

The transfer was approved in four days. Hartmann himself signed it. He came down to her unit to tell her in person, smiling, and said *I think this is a wonderful fit for you*, and Lihua said *thank you*, and Hartmann said *it's good to have someone we can trust in that unit*, and Lihua heard, beneath the words, what he meant. *Thank you for going where you can do the least damage.*

She thanked him again and went back to her station.

That night, she wrote in her leather book: *The next one will not be filed as cardiac.*

She was wrong about that. The next one was filed as cardiac. So was the one after that. So was the one in 2068 that her supervisor called her about personally and that she didn't file at all, and that she didn't write in her leather book either, because by 2068 she'd learned that some things weren't safe to write down.

She continued working in the unit that would later be called bound-care. She continued raising her son, whose name was Wei, whom she'd told, when he was old enough to ask, that his biological mother could not raise him and had asked Lihua to. She did not tell him the name of the woman. She did not tell him, then or later, that the woman was alive. That the woman was nearby. That the woman would, by 2080, be three floors above the unit where Lihua now worked, in a private room, in a bed that didn't need to be changed often because the patient didn't move much.

She would care for that woman, in fourteen years, with no record on either of their charts of how they were connected. She would call the patient by the patient's institutional designation. The patient would not call Lihua anything, because the patient had stopped calling anyone anything in 2076.

But that was the future. In 2066 she was forty-one. She had a six-year-old. She had just filed her first report that no one wanted, and she had just been told, kindly, not to file the next one.

She kept the leather book. She didn't throw it away. She moved it, every few years, when she moved apartments. By the time she died, in 2089, it would have been read by precisely one other person. That person would find it among her effects and recognize the handwriting and sit down at her kitchen table and weep for an hour and then begin to read it from the beginning.

That person would be Wei.

That was the beginning of the archive.
